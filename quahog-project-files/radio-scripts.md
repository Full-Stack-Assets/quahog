Here's a true story. Fella calls me last week, says, Sully, my wife says it's the radio or her. I said, pal, I'm gonna miss her. Stay greasy out there.

You're ridin' with Sully on WHALE ninety-two point one, and I'll let you in on the secret to a long life on the South Coast: keep your tank above a quarter, keep your mouth shut at the rotary, and never trust a forecast from a guy who's never owned a boat.

Alright, I'm gettin' the wrap-it-up sign from the kid in the booth. Crank it loud enough on the way home and you won't hear the muffler comin' loose. That's just science. That's just good livin'.

Salty air, loud guitars, and nobody tellin' you to turn it down. This is WHALE, ninety-two point one, the Whaling City's home for classic rock. Sully's outta here. Be good to each other, and I'll be back tomorrow — if the bridge ever closes and I can get to work.

Alright, that's the program, my voice is goin' and my coffee's cold. Be good to each other out there, leave the seagulls alone, and for the love of Pete — USE YA BLINKAH. Buddy Mello, fourteen-eighty AM, The Rage. Signin' off before I say somethin' the FCC writes me a letter about.

## IRON MIKE FONTAINE — The Anvil, WBOX ("City of Champions")
Voice: deep, slow, gravelly, commanding old fight trainer. Override: `ELEVENLABS_MIKE_VOICE` (Shaun - Boston).

This is The Anvil. Iron Mike Fontaine. City of Champions, baby, and don't you forget it.

You wanna be great? You really wanna be great? Then you get up at five. Before the city wakes up. Before the excuses wake up. You run the hill, you hit the bag till it begs, and you do it again tomorrow when nobody's clappin'.

They ask me, Mike, what's the secret. There's no secret. There's a heavy bag, a jump rope, and a clock that don't care about your feelings. That's the whole sport. That's the whole life.

Champion City Gym, corner spot down off the avenue. Smells like liniment and ambition. The door's always open. The excuses stay outside on the sidewalk where they belong.

I had a kid come in last week. Soft hands, soft eyes, lookin' for somebody to tell him he was special. Two weeks of roadwork later? Different animal. Different man. That's the work. The work doesn't lie to you.

Discipline ain't punishment. You hear me out there? Discipline is love. It's love for who you're gonna become. The man you could be is countin' on the man you are right now to do the work. Don't let him down.

I been hit by guys twice my size and I'm still standin' here talkin' to ya. So don't tell me a Monday's gonna stop you. Don't tell me the alarm clock's too early. Fear's just a bell. It rings, you answer, you go to work.

They knocked this city down, didn't they. The mills closed. The boats slowed. The jobs went south. And what'd we do? Tell me what we did. We got UP. Eight count, nine count, and we got up off the canvas. That's who we are. That's the South Coast. We get up.

You skip roadwork, the ring knows. You can lie to me, you can lie to your trainer, you can lie to the girl, but the ring always knows. It can smell a quitter through the ropes. Don't be a guy the ring's ashamed of.

Don't talk to me about talent. Talent sleeps in. Talent thinks it's already arrived. I'll take the hungry kid over the gifted kid every single time, twice on Sunday. Hunger shows up early and stays late.

My old trainer told me somethin' I never forgot. He said, Mike, your fists are honest. Everything else in this world will lie to ya — the scale, the mirror, the people who say they love ya — but your fists, they tell the truth about the work you put in. Or didn't.

You get dropped? Good. GOOD. Now we find out what you're really made of. Anybody can fight when they're ahead. I wanna see what you do off the floor, lungs burnin', ears ringin', the whole crowd against ya. That's the only round that counts.

The body's a tool and pain's the whetstone. You sharpen, or you rust. There's no third option. There's no standin' still. Every day you're gettin' better or you're gettin' worse, so which one's it gonna be, champ?

Somebody asked me on the line, Mike, did you ever lose? Course I lost. I lost plenty. And losin' taught me everything that winnin' never could. Winnin' makes you stupid and soft. Losin' makes you pay attention. Be grateful for the nights it didn't go your way.

Three rounds left in ya. Always. When you got nothin' left, when the tank reads empty and your legs are gone — you got three rounds. You dig down and you find 'em. They're in there. They're always in there. I promise you that.

I don't believe in "can't." I knew a fella named Cantt once, years ago, down the gym. Glass jaw. Quit in the fourth every time. So don't you be a Cantt. Don't even say the word in my buildin'.

Lights out, hands up, chin down. We go again tomorrow, and the day after that, and the day after that. That's the only plan there's ever been. This is The Anvil, this is Iron Mike Fontaine, and remember — we forge, or we fold. And around here, we don't fold.

## TIA CONCEIÇÃO — Maré Alta, 105.3 ("high tide on your dial")
Voice: warm, lilting, maternal, bilingual Portuguese/Cape Verdean woman. Override: `ELEVENLABS_TIA_VOICE`.

Put the bacalhau to soak, open the window, let the salt air in. This one's for the avós — for the grandmothers who kept the whole family together with a wooden spoon and a look that could stop a grown man cold.

For everyone workin' the docks this mornin' — força, my dears. Be careful out there on that water. The sea, she gives, and the sea, she takes, so you respect her and you come home to your families. We're sayin' a little prayer for you right here.

This one is from Conceição in the North End to her son out fishin' on the Georges Bank. Querido, your mother says come home safe, and she says you don't call enough, and she's right, you don't. Call your mother. All of you. Call your mothers.

Cape Verde, the Açores, Madeira, the continent — todos juntos, all of us together, right here on the South Coast. One people, one tide. We crossed an ocean and we brought the music with us, and as long as we play it, home is never that far away.

The Feast is comin', my loves. The biggest Portuguese feast in all of America, right here. The lights over the field, the carne de espeto on the grill, the smell of it comin' all the way down the avenue. I'll see you under the lights. Save me a malasada. Save me two.

A little morna now for the saudade — for that ache you carry for a place you maybe never even saw, only heard about in your grandmother's stories. That's the most Portuguese feeling there is. To miss a home that lives only in here, in the heart. Vamos sentir juntos.

Don't forget — Sunday is your avó's name day. Flowers. Real ones, from a shop, with the stems still wet. Not the ones from the gas station, querido. She will know. She always knows. That woman knows everything.

Need a new fado record, or a little funaná to wake up the whole house? Maré Alta Records, downtown, end of the block. Tell them a Tia mandou — tell them Tia sent you — and they'll treat you like family, which on the South Coast means they'll argue with you about the price and then give you the better one anyway.

A song for the mothers now. For the women who worked two jobs, three jobs, scrubbed the floors and the fish houses and still found the strength to dance at the feast on Saturday night. That is not weakness, my loves. That is the strongest thing I know. Obrigada, mães. We see you.

Rain comin' in off the bay later, they say. Good. The garden needs it. My tomatoes need it. And honestly, so does my soul. We'll put on a little coladeira and dance in the kitchen anyway. Who's gonna stop us? The rain? Let it come.

High tide down at the hurricane barrier this mornin', the gulls cryin' over the fish house, the whole city smellin' of salt and diesel and somebody's linguiça. People complain about that smell. Not me. That's the smell of everybody workin'. That's the smell of home.

This next one goes out to the young ones, the filhos and filhas born here, who roll their eyes when the old songs come on. Listen, querido. One day you'll be far from here and one of these songs will come on somewhere unexpected, and it'll bring you right back to this kitchen, this morning, your grandmother's voice. Keep these songs. They're yours now.

For the heartbroken, a little morna. For the hopeful, a little coladeira. And tonight, my loves, we have both, because that's life, isn't it — both at the same time, always. The ache and the dancing. You don't get one without the other.

From the South End to Clark's Point, from the bridge to the boats, from the islands to this little studio with the window that doesn't close right — boa tarde, my beautiful people. You are not alone this morning. The whole community is right here with you on the dial.

Keep the faith, keep the family close, and keep the music playin' loud enough that the neighbors complain. That's how they know we're still here. This is Maré Alta, cento e cinco, and this is your Tia. Até logo, my loves. Go be good to somebody today.
